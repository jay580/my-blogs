Why Your Internet Slows Down

Congestion, packet loss, TCP's sawtooth — a plain-language breakdown of why your connection betrays you at the worst moments.

Jay·Networks & Systems·TCP · ISP · Wi-Fi · Congestion

You're in the middle of something important — a video call, a file download, a livestream — and the connection dies. The wheel spins. You restart the router. Sometimes it helps. Usually it doesn't. This is not random. It is the deterministic result of physics, protocol design, and shared infrastructure colliding at once.

01 — The Basics

How Data Actually Travels

Every file you download, every message you send, every video frame you watch arrives as packets — small chunks of data, typically 1,500 bytes each, fired across a network. Your browser doesn't request one giant blob. It opens a TCP connection and requests thousands of packets in parallel.

TCP — Transmission Control Protocol — is the invisible engine beneath almost all internet traffic. It guarantees delivery: if a packet is lost, TCP detects it and retransmits. But this reliability comes at a cost. To avoid overwhelming the network, TCP must constantly ask: how fast can I safely send?

"The internet wasn't built to be fast. It was built to be resilient. Speed is an emergent property — and congestion is the price we pay for it."

02 — Slow Start

TCP Starts Cautiously

When a TCP connection opens, it doesn't immediately fire packets at full speed. It starts with a congestion window (cwnd) of 1 — sends one packet and waits for an ACK. On success, it doubles: 1 → 2 → 4 → 8 → 16. This is Slow Start.

Slow Start isn't slow because it's cautious about speed — it's slow because it begins from zero. Doubling every round-trip time (RTT), a connection on a 50ms link reaches 64 packets in just 300ms. Once cwnd hits a threshold (ssthresh), TCP switches to Congestion Avoidance: it now grows by just 1 per RTT. Linear growth, not exponential.

03 — The Sawtooth

What Congestion Looks Like

TCP detects congestion when packets are lost. The receiver stops sending ACKs. After a timeout, TCP assumes the network is overloaded and cuts cwnd drastically — sometimes back to 1, sometimes to half. Then Slow Start begins again.

Plot cwnd over time and you get a sawtooth wave: climb, climb, cliff, climb again. This is TCP's AIMD algorithm — Additive Increase, Multiplicative Decrease. Grow slowly, shrink fast. Conservative by design.

This is why your connection stutters rhythmically during congestion — TCP is repeatedly climbing and falling, probing for the network's true capacity.

04 — Seven Culprits

The Real Reasons Your Internet Slows Down

01
Network Congestion at the ISP

Your ISP's backhaul links have finite capacity. When millions stream simultaneously — peak hours, 8–11 PM — those links saturate and every TCP connection starts dropping packets.

02
Router Bufferbloat

Modern routers have large memory buffers. When congested, packets pile up instead of being dropped — causing massive latency spikes before TCP even detects a problem.

03
Wi-Fi Interference

Wi-Fi is a shared medium. Every device on your 2.4GHz band — your neighbour's router, a microwave — causes collisions and retransmissions that TCP reads as congestion.

04
Long Physical Distance

Light travels through fibre at ~200,000 km/s. Mumbai to New York takes ~150ms minimum. TCP's cwnd cannot grow fast enough on high-latency links — physics wins.

05
Server Overload

When millions hit a server simultaneously — a product launch, viral post — CPU, memory, and network saturate. Response times climb from 10ms to 10 seconds.

06
Too Many Open Connections

40 browser tabs plus system updates means hundreds of competing cwnd windows trying to grow simultaneously — they starve each other out.

07
Deep Packet Inspection / Throttling

ISPs can legally throttle specific traffic types — video streaming, torrents. They inspect packet headers, identify the category, and artificially inject delay. Intentional, not accidental.

05 — OS Perspective

What Your Operating System Sees

From the OS perspective, congestion control is a scheduling and resource allocation problem. The kernel must fairly allocate the network interface — a shared hardware resource — among hundreds of competing processes, each with its own TCP connection and cwnd.

The OS uses traffic shaping (queueing disciplines — QDisc in Linux) and socket buffers (SO_SNDBUF, SO_RCVBUF) to manage this. When the outgoing queue fills up, the OS drops packets from its own buffer — before they even leave your machine.

💡 OS Concept — Resource Allocation

TCP congestion control is distributed resource allocation without a central scheduler. Every sender independently self-limits based on local observations — exactly like processes in a preemptive OS sharing CPU time, except there is no kernel to enforce fairness. TCP's AIMD algorithm converges to fairness mathematically.

06 — TCP Evolution

How TCP Got Smarter Over Time

The original TCP (Tahoe, 1988) crashed cwnd back to 1 on every loss event — brutal but safe. TCP Reno (1990) introduced Fast Recovery: drop cwnd to ssthresh and skip back into Congestion Avoidance instead of restarting. Fewer wasted RTTs recovering from loss.

Modern variants go further. CUBIC (Linux since 2006) uses a cubic function to grow cwnd more aggressively on high-bandwidth links. BBR (Google, 2016) abandons loss-based inference entirely — it directly models bandwidth and RTT to find the optimal sending rate, dramatically better on high-latency links like satellite internet.

07 — What You Can Do

Actually Fixing It

📶
Switch to 5GHz Wi-Fi

Less interference, higher speed. Eliminates most packet loss from Wi-Fi collisions in dense environments.

🔌
Use Ethernet

Wired connections eliminate wireless retransmission entirely. TCP gets a clean, low-loss channel.

🌐
Use a CDN-backed Service

CDNs place servers close to you, reducing RTT. Smaller RTT = faster cwnd growth = more throughput.

⏰
Avoid Peak Hours

8–11 PM is when ISP backhaul congestion peaks. Schedule large downloads for off-peak hours.

🔁
Restart Your Router

Clears NAT tables, resets buffer queues. Often resolves bufferbloat and stale connection states.

🛡️
Use a VPN (for throttling)

Encrypts packets so your ISP can't inspect traffic type — defeats application-layer throttling.

08 — The Bigger Picture

The Internet Is Self-Healing

No single entity controls internet congestion. There is no global traffic manager, no central coordinator. Five billion users, sending trillions of packets per second, are regulated entirely by billions of independent TCP stacks running the same simple algorithm — probe, back off, probe again.

So next time the spinning wheel appears, remember: that is not the internet failing. That is TCP doing exactly what it was designed to do in 1988. Backing off. Waiting. Probing for space. The slowdown is the feature.

"The Slowdown is the Feature."

TCP's congestion control is the reason the internet doesn't collapse under its own weight. Every backoff, every cwnd reset, every sawtooth is the network regulating itself — without a single central authority.
